Metal buildings, whether they are used as warehouses, garages, or workshops, are durable and versatile structures. However, without proper insulation, they can become uncomfortable and inefficient. If you're looking to protect your investment and improve the usability of your metal building, insulation is essential. This guide will walk you through why insulation is important, the materials commonly used, and a step-by-step guide for how to insulate a metal building.

Why Insulating a Metal Building Is Important

Temperature Regulation

Metal buildings, by their nature, are susceptible to significant temperature fluctuations. During the summer months, the heat from the sun can cause metal structures to become excessively hot, while in the winter, these buildings can get uncomfortably cold and form condensation inside. Metal building insulation acts as a barrier to regulate the internal temperature, ensuring a more comfortable environment year-round.

Energy Cost Savings

One of the primary benefits of insulating a metal building is the potential for energy cost savings. Insulation minimizes the transfer of heat, reducing the load on heating and cooling systems. This results in less energy consumption, leading to lower utility bills and making your building more energy-efficient overall. High quality metal building insulation like BlueTex™ can help your building be more energy efficient all year long.

Protection Against Moisture

Condensation is a common issue in metal buildings, especially in areas with high humidity or cold climates. Without insulation, moisture can accumulate on the metal surfaces, leading to corrosion, equipment damage, and structural damage. A good thermal vapor barrier should create a seal that prevents condensation, protecting both the structure and the items stored inside from moisture-related damage. Using BlueTex™ insulation as a vapor barrier to manage moisture in a non-conditioned or semi-conditioned metal building is a great way to fix condensation issues, especially in colder climates.

Noise Reduction

If your metal building is located near a busy area or used as a workshop, noise can become a nuisance. Insulating the building can help reduce sound transmission from the outside and dampen the noise inside, providing a quieter and more peaceful working environment.

Increased Building Longevity

Properly insulating a metal building can extend its lifespan. By controlling temperature extremes and preventing moisture buildup, insulation helps reduce wear and tear on the building's structural components, preserving the integrity of the building for many years to come.

Common Insulation Materials for Metal Buildings

There are several types of insulation materials that can be used for metal buildings, each with its advantages and disadvantages. Here's an overview of the most common options:

Fiberglass Insulation

Fiberglass is one of the most popular insulation materials used in metal buildings due to its affordability and ease of installation. It consists of fine glass fibers woven into a mat, providing effective thermal resistance. However, it can lose effectiveness if it becomes wet, so proper moisture control is essential. Fiberglass insulation also poses health risks, as exposing your skin and airways to glass fibers is dangerous.

Spray Foam Insulation

Spray foam insulation expands to fill every gap, making it an excellent option for providing airtight insulation. It also acts as a vapor barrier, which helps prevent moisture buildup. However, it is more expensive than other types of insulation and requires professional installation, which makes it out of reach for many customers. Additionally, customers complain about the smell from the off-gassing that occurs with spray foam insulation.

Rigid Foam (Foam Board)

Rigid foam board is durable and provides good R-value in a compact sheet. Foam board insulation is often used in walls and ceilings for metal buildings, though not usually alone. Rigid foam board is moisture-resistant, making it a good choice in humid environments. However, they can be more challenging to install than other options and they typically are paired with other insulation.

Reflective Insulation

Reflective insulation, such as BlueTex™, is designed to reflect radiant heat rather than absorb it. This type of insulation is highly effective in hot climates where the primary concern is keeping the building cool, but it also proves to be beneficial in cold climates trying to hold heat inside a building. BlueTex™ is a high-quality metal building insulation. Even though it is thinner than foam board or fiberglass insulation, it’s tear-proof, so it’s very easy to install in all conditions. BlueTex™ metal building insulation is also a vapor barrier and air barrier, so it helps control moisture from forming inside the building. BlueTex™ comes in two thicknesses, one for warmer climates and one for colder climates. For the customer that wants to DIY their metal building insulation, BlueTex™ is a great, affordable and accessible insulation option.

Step-by-Step Guide to Insulating a Metal Building

Step 1: Assess Your Insulation Needs

Before starting your insulation project, assess the specific needs of your building. Consider the climate in your area, the building's intended use, and any potential issues like moisture buildup or extreme temperatures. Also, consider if you plan to heat or cool the building and, if so, how often. This thoughtful assessment will help you determine which type of insulation is best suited for your building for the best results.

Step 2: Choose the Right Insulation Material

Based on your assessment, choose the insulation material that best meets your needs. For example, if you're in a hot climate, reflective insulation like BlueTex™ would be ideal and perform better than fiberglass. In colder climates, you might opt for doubling up and pair BlueTex™ with an insulation that has higher R-value, like fiberglass or spray foam. This combined effort works best for heat retention if you’re heating on a consistent basis.

Step 3: Install Insulation in the Walls

Start by insulating the walls of your metal building, since these are usually the most assessable and easiest. Your reflective barrier should always be closest to the outside of the wall cavity, with an air gap between the sheet metal and the foil surface. Most people attach it to the face of the frame on the inside, leaving the wall cavities empty. If you don’t plan to heat/cool your building more than a few hours a week, this is suitable and effective. However, if you plan to use air conditioning or heating more often than that (like 20+ hours a week), you need to install the BlueTex™ in between the frame. When you do this, you will use spacers to achieve an air gap, or you may have horizontal purlins that you can attach the BlueTex™ directly into to keep it off the exterior sheathing. Any secondary R-value or wall covering would go up after the BlueTex™ is installed.

Step 4: Insulate the Roof

The roof is one of the most important areas to insulate, as it’s often the primary source of heat transfer. Reflective insulation or rigid foam boards can be installed beneath the roof panels, allowing for an air gap of at least ½” between the roof panels and the insulation. This gap can be vented or sealed; it won’t change how the insulation works. If you plan to use spray foam on the roof, the best combination is to use a radiant barrier closest to the roofing panels and then apply your spray foam directly to the underside of the insulation to create an airtight seal.

Step 5: Floor Insulation Options

While floors are not typically insulated in metal buildings, you can still consider adding a layer of rigid foam board insulation beneath a concrete floor or using an insulated floor covering to help with temperature regulation. Radiant floor heating installed in a building floor is also very popular in colder climates.

Step 6: Seal Windows and Doors

To maximize the effectiveness of your insulation, ensure that windows and doors are properly sealed and can open and close easily. Use weatherstripping and caulk to close any gaps and prevent drafts, which can undermine the insulating properties of your building.

Insulating an Existing Metal Building

Retrofitting Insulation

If your metal building already has insulation but it’s not performing well or it’s gotten dirty or ripped or is crumbling after years of use, retrofitting a new layer of insulation might be necessary. This involves adding new insulation over existing materials, usually without removing them. BlueTex™ insulation is a great option for retrofitting, as it can easily be installed over old, dirty insulation without the need to remove it first. We call this the Cover System and we have an install guide for how to do this on both the roofline of your building and the walls of your building. Retrofitting is an easy DIY application that can be done in stages or all at once. The result is a refreshed building interior that performs better year-round.

Cost-Effective Insulation Options

If budget is a concern, reflective insulation is the most cost-effective option that provides the greatest impact on performance. While spray foam may offer the best R-value, it’s also the most expensive. The majority of buildings aren’t being heated/cooled 24/7 either, so spray foam isn’t necessarily the best choice. For non-conditioned buildings (or semi-conditioned buildings) – spaces you’re not heating and cooling every single day – radiant barriers provide the most dramatic improvement. They block 97% of that radiant heat and keep it out of the building right away. They also have the advantage of working in the cold climates too, helping heat stay inside when a customer is heating the building on especially cold days/nights. For DIY enthusiasts, reflective insulation is easy to install and highly effective, making it a popular choice for high quality metal building insulation.

Additional Tips for Insulating Metal Buildings

Preventing Moisture Buildup

Moisture control is critical in metal buildings. Consider using BlueTex™ metal building insulation as your vapor barrier. You can also use moisture-resistant insulation like spray foam or rigid foam boards to prevent condensation issues. No matter which product you choose, how you install the product will ultimately determine how well you can prevent moisture. See our Complete Guide to Moisture Management here.

Fire Safety Considerations

Always check the fire rating of the insulation materials you choose. Some insulation materials are more flammable than others, so it's important to select fire-resistant materials, especially in buildings where fire hazards may exist. Remember, there’s no such thing as a 100% fireproof insulation, even if some companies claim as much.
