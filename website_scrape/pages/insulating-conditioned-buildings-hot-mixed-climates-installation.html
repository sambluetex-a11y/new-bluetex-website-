If you have an existing building that you heat and cool regularly, just like a home, this install page will explain the best way to insulate, assuming the building is already constructed. If this is a new building, that has not been built yet, you should follow the guidance for NEW CONSTRUCTION to add your radiant barrier foil layer, and then you will add your r-value to the inside as shown below.

Fully Conditioned Building Conversions (Retrofit)

Fully conditioned means you are heating/cooling the building 24/7 to maintain a constant temp at all times. Common applications for this install method include container living conversions, barndominiums/shouses, recreational/game rooms, and office space. Another example would be a non-conditioned building that has a sectioned off space for constant heating/cooling, like an office or an apartment.

Use Radiant Barrier + R-value Insulation (+ possibly another vapor barrier)

If heat rejection is your primary goal in a fully conditioned building , using a combination of radiant barrier and traditional insulation is the best approach.

You always want to get your radiant barrier closest to the outside of the building (near the sheet metal), with the proper air gap on the foil side. To do this, you will need to either use a spacer (or girt) or attach the foil to the frame/purlin about 0.5"+ away from the metal. You can make this air space bigger than 0.5" but not smaller. This method is most effective at keeping heat out, and it also works very well for heat retention in the building during colder seasons too. If heat retention (cold climates) is your primary goal, see the install page here .

After you add the radiant barrier with the air space between the metal and the foil, then you add the R-value insulation toward the interior of the living space . This can be spray foam, batt insulation, foam board, etc. and you want to fill the cavities as full as you can for maximum benefit.

Because metal buildings are different than residential homes built with wood and siding/stone, you will need an interior vapor barrier to ensure the interior air doesn't infiltrate the wall cavities. This is why we recommend closed cell spray foam insulation in this application. However you can use another type of R-value but you will need to add an additional vapor barrier (this is in addition to your foil vapor barrier layer) over the walls , before your interior wall finish. This is especially helpful in cold/wet climates.

If your building is not fully conditioned (meaning non-conditioned or semi-conditioned, see the install guide here ).

Basics of the Installation

The basic layers are as follows:

Exterior metal --> air gap (created using spacers) --> foil surface of your radiant barrier product (this can also be a vapor barrier) --> R-value (right up against your radiant barrier for a snug fit) --> optional vapor barrier if not using spray foam --> interior wall finish.

How It Works

In a typical building, the outside “skin” can easily reach over 150ºF in direct sunlight. Once the skin heats up, the heat flows via direct conduction through the walls and ceiling. When you create an airspace between the skin and the foil surface, this FORCES most of the heat coming in to the building to CONVERT to radiant heat, in order to cross the airspace. Radiant heat is actually EASIER to control than conductive heat, since it can be REFLECTED just like light*! Once the heat is converted to radiant heat, we OWN IT! We can reflect the energy away from the regular insulation with the foil, which makes the regular insulation more effective.

Simply put, think of your building like a refrigerator sitting in the direct sun. Do we agree that the refrigerator would be more efficient if we could move it from under the sun and put it in the shade? Yes! In this same way, radiant barrier basically acts like shade, making any traditional insulation more effective by reducing the heat load before it ever reaches the insulation inside the walls and ceilings.

*Radiant heat/ infrared radiation is actually the invisible part of the light spectrum.

Layers

When dealing with conditioned buildings (air conditioned/ heated) think in terms of layers and air-tightness .

Basically, you want to create a well-insulated box that is airtight, and you want to wrap that box with radiant barrier. The radiant barrier will act as the first line of defense against radiant heat, while the standard insulation (with R-Value) will act as the second line of defense against conductive heat flow. Your layers will look something similar to this:

    Exterior Metal Skin Air Gap (created by furring strips of any material) RADIANT BARRIER Foil (solid or perforated version, depending on the R-value type) R-value (foam board, spray foam, batt insulation, etc.) - right up against the inside-facing foil layer Wall/Ceiling Finish (this can be a layer of foam board, Sheetrock, OSB, peg board, sheet metal, or even another layer of BlueTex™, as seen below!)

Also, when trying to stop heat gain, you want the foil layer closest to the OUTSIDE of the shell (the building) so you block the heat FIRST, then use your R-value to slow down the remaining heat.

    Air Flow/Ventilation

    Air flow and ventilation are great for moving the heat out of an area and keeping things dry in general. Remember that in a wall cavity and in some cathedral ceilings, there's not really any air flow and that's ok ! If you have an air-tight layer in the cavity, this air won't hurt anything and you don't have to vent it. So, the foil only needs an air space to reflect radiant heat, and that air doesn't necessarily have to be vented .

    When you install radiant barrier in a metal building this way, and add your R-value and seal up the wall/roof cavities airtight, you'll have a system that works better than anything else to help the building stay comfortable and efficient in the hot summer months.
